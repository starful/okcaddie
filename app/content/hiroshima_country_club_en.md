## Course Overview

Hiroshima Country Club, established in 1960, boasts 36 championship holes split into two distinct 18-hole layouts: the East Course (Higashi) and the West Course (Nishi). The East Course, designed by Shunsuke Kato, and the West Course, by Osamu Maejima, each play around 6,800 to 7,000 yards from the back tees for a par of 72.

As a prominent "Forest Course," both layouts are characterized by mature, tree-lined fairways that demand precision and strategic shot-making, often featuring significant elevation changes typical of the region. Fairways typically utilize Zoysia grass, providing a firm playing surface, while the greens are usually Bentgrass, known for their consistent roll.

## Layout & Strategy

The East Course's 5th hole, a par 4, presents a challenging dogleg right with an uphill approach to the green. Your tee shot needs to hug the left side of the fairway to open up the green, avoiding the dense trees on the right that penalize anything pushed too far. The second shot often requires an extra club to clear a hidden bunker guarding the front-right of the green, with putting lines frequently breaking towards the lower side of the hill.

On the West Course, the 14th hole is a memorable par 3, usually played from an elevated tee across a ravine to a well-bunkered green. Selecting the correct club is paramount, often a 6 or 7 iron depending on wind conditions, as short shots will find the ravine and long shots face tricky recovery. Pay close attention to subtle breaks on the multi-tiered green, which can be difficult to read.

The 18th hole on the East Course is a formidable par 5 finisher, requiring two precise shots before considering an approach to the green. The tee shot is tight, with dense woods flanking both sides, and the fairway narrows considerably around the 250-yard mark. A strategic layup short of the final green complex is often the wisest play, avoiding deep bunkers and potential water hazards that await overly ambitious third shots.

## Practical Info

Green fees at Hiroshima Country Club typically range from ¥15,000 to ¥25,000 on weekdays, increasing to ¥20,000 to ¥35,000 on weekends and holidays. As a private club, outside play is generally restricted; prospective guests usually require a member's introduction or must play as a guest of a member. However, limited public tee times may be available through specific golf travel agencies or during off-peak periods.

Play is predominantly cart-based, with caddies often mandatory or highly recommended for the best experience, especially for first-time visitors navigating the course's nuances. The dress code is standard for a Japanese private club: collared shirts and golf slacks/shorts are required on the course, with no denim permitted. Smart casual attire is expected in the clubhouse, and a jacket may be appropriate for formal dining.

## Access

Hiroshima Country Club is located in Higashihiroshima, approximately a 15-minute drive from Higashi-Hiroshima Station, which is served by the Sanyo Shinkansen line. From central Hiroshima City, the drive typically takes 45 to 60 minutes, depending on traffic conditions. Ample parking is available on-site for those traveling by car.

## Clubhouse Notes

The clubhouse offers extensive amenities befitting a top-tier private facility, featuring spacious and impeccably maintained locker rooms. Guests can enjoy a traditional Japanese communal bath (ofuro) for relaxation after their round, providing a soothing experience. The clubhouse restaurant serves a range of local and Western dishes; a recommended specialty often includes local seafood preparations like fresh oyster dishes or anago meshi (sea eel rice).

## Tips

The ideal months to play Hiroshima Country Club are typically April-May and October-November, offering pleasant temperatures and scenic conditions with autumn foliage. Be aware that Japanese greens can have more subtle breaks than expected, so always take extra time with your reads. Summer (July-August) can bring extreme heat and humidity, along with a risk of typhoons, while winter (December-February) may see frost delays or occasional snow, particularly on higher elevations. Expect peak season pricing and limited availability during popular spring and autumn weekends.
