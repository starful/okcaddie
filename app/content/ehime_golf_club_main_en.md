## Course Overview

Ehime Golf Club Main, established in the late 1980s amidst Japan's golf boom, presents a quintessential mountain-style golfing experience. This 18-hole, par 72 layout spans approximately 6,600 to 6,900 yards from the back tees, offering a challenging yet fair test of golf. While specific designer information is not widely publicized, the course reflects the thoughtful design trends of its era, integrating naturally with the undulating terrain.

The course features well-maintained bentgrass greens, offering a consistent putting surface that demands precision. Fairways are typically a blend of zoysia grass, common in Japanese courses, providing a firm base for iron play. Its overall character is defined by mature, dense forests and significant elevation changes, requiring strategic shot placement rather than brute force.

Golfers visiting Ehime Golf Club Main can expect a journey through a serene natural environment, emphasizing value without compromising on the quality of the playing experience. The course delivers on its promise of a forest golf adventure, with each hole presenting a unique challenge framed by the scenic beauty of the Ehime mountains.

## Layout & Strategy

The layout at Ehime Golf Club Main consistently demands thoughtful course management, with tree-lined fairways and elevation shifts defining many shots. Strategic positioning off the tee is paramount to setting up manageable approaches, often to greens that feature subtle but challenging breaks. The mountain-style design ensures that few holes play flat, adding an extra layer of complexity to club selection.

Consider the 4th hole, a par 4 playing around 380-410 yards, which features a demanding tee shot over a gentle valley to a tightening fairway framed by tall pines. A well-struck drive to the left-center leaves a clearer approach, avoiding an intimidating bunker guarding the right side of the green. The green itself slopes gently from back to front, making downhill putts particularly quick.

The 7th hole, a scenic par 3 ranging from 160-185 yards, often plays significantly downhill, requiring players to take at least one club less than the yardage suggests. A large pond flanks the left side of the green, while a steep embankment catches any shots pulled too far right. The putting surface here is deceptively flat, but read for subtle breaks towards the water.

Moving to the back nine, the 13th hole is a challenging par 5, approximately 520-550 yards, featuring a significant dogleg right around a heavily forested hill. The tee shot demands a draw to cut the corner, but an overly aggressive line risks finding the dense trees. Laying up short of a fairway bunker on the second shot is often the smart play, leaving a short iron into a two-tiered green protected by deep bunkers on both sides.

Finally, the 17th hole, a tricky par 4 measuring 360-390 yards, often plays uphill towards the green after an initial downhill drive. The tee shot must avoid a series of bunkers on the left side of the fairway, funneling players towards the right rough. The approach shot requires careful club selection to account for the uphill trajectory, aiming for the front of a deep green that slopes sharply from back to front.

## Conditions & Seasonality

The playing conditions at Ehime Golf Club Main generally reflect the meticulous standards found across many Japanese courses. Fairways are kept neat, and the bentgrass greens typically roll true, though their speed can vary depending on the season and recent maintenance. The "Value for Money" tag for this course speaks to the excellent upkeep relative to its approachable green fees.

The best months for golfing in Ehime are during the spring (April to early June) and autumn (October to mid-November), when temperatures are mild and rainfall is less frequent. Summer months can bring high humidity and occasional heavy rain, which can make the course play longer and softer. Winter sees cooler temperatures, and while Ehime generally avoids heavy snow, frosty mornings can sometimes delay tee times.

Pace of play during weekdays is typically efficient, with most rounds completing within four to four-and-a-half hours. Weekends, especially during peak seasons, can see a slightly slower pace due to higher traffic, though Japanese etiquette generally encourages players to keep up. It's advisable to book early for weekend tee times to secure preferred slots, particularly for morning rounds.

Wind conditions can be a factor on this mountain-style course, as swirling gusts might unexpectedly affect shots, especially on elevated tees or exposed fairways. Rain, when it occurs, typically drains well from the fairways, but the dense forest can make finding errant shots more challenging. Staying updated on local weather forecasts is always a sensible approach before heading out.

## Dress Code & On-Course Rules

Adherence to the dress code at Ehime Golf Club Main is consistent with standard Japanese golf etiquette, which tends to be more conservative than some Western clubs. Collared shirts are mandatory for both men and women, tucked into trousers or appropriate golf shorts. Denim jeans, t-shirts, tracksuits, and overly casual attire are not permitted on the course or in the clubhouse dining areas.

Footwear should consist of soft-spiked or spikeless golf shoes, as metal spikes are generally discouraged or prohibited to protect greens. During cooler months, sweaters or golf-specific outerwear are acceptable, but hoodies are typically not allowed. These guidelines are in place to maintain a respectful atmosphere and ensure a pleasant experience for all patrons.

On the course, mobile phone usage is expected to be discreet and kept to a minimum. Ringers should be silenced, and conversations are generally restricted to emergency situations or conducted quietly away from other players. The clubhouse may have designated areas for phone calls, but active use on the fairways or greens is generally frowned upon.

Pace of play is a significant aspect of Japanese golf etiquette. Groups are expected to maintain the flow of play and keep up with the group in front. Players should be ready to hit when it's their turn, avoid excessive practice swings, and move promptly between shots and holes. Retrieving lost balls should be done quickly, and if a ball is unfindable, dropping another and moving on is often preferred to holding up play. Additionally, raking bunkers and repairing pitch marks are expected courtesies.

## Access

Ehime Golf Club Main is situated in Uchiko, Ehime Prefecture, offering a serene escape that requires a bit of travel but rewards visitors with tranquility. The nearest significant train station is Uchiko Station, accessible via the JR Yosan Line from Matsuyama. From Uchiko Station, the course is typically a 15-20 minute taxi ride, which can be arranged in advance or found at the station.

For those flying into the region, Matsuyama Airport (MYJ) is the most convenient entry point. From Matsuyama Airport, it's approximately a 50-60 minute drive to Ehime Golf Club Main, either by rental car or pre-booked shuttle/taxi. Rental cars are widely available at the airport and offer the most flexibility for exploring the region.

Driving from major cities in the Kansai region involves a longer journey. From Osaka or Kobe, a direct drive would typically take 5-6 hours, crossing several impressive bridges like the Seto Ohashi. A more common approach is to take the Shinkansen to Okayama, then connect to a local train or rent a car, or even take a ferry from Osaka to Matsuyama and then drive. From Hiroshima, a convenient option is to take a ferry directly to Matsuyama Port (approximately 2.5-3 hours) and then drive for about an hour to the course.

Driving directly from Tokyo is generally impractical due to the vast distance, often taking over 10 hours. Flying into Matsuyama Airport from Haneda or Narita is the recommended method for visitors coming from the greater Tokyo area. The course provides ample free parking for those who choose to drive, making car access straightforward once you arrive in the Ehime region.

## Clubhouse & Dining

The clubhouse at Ehime Golf Club Main, while not overtly luxurious, is functional, clean, and perfectly suited for its purpose, providing all the necessary amenities for a comfortable day of golf. Locker rooms are well-maintained, offering individual lockers for changing and storing belongings securely. These facilities are clean and spacious enough to accommodate the daily flow of golfers.

A highlight of the clubhouse experience, typical of Japanese golf clubs, is the large public bath, or *ofuro*. After a round on the mountain course, golfers can unwind in the spacious communal bath, which is meticulously cleaned and offers a relaxing soak. While not a natural *onsen* (hot spring), it provides a refreshing way to soothe tired muscles and is an integral part of the Japanese golf tradition.

The club's restaurant offers a selection of straightforward and satisfying Japanese and Western-style dishes, serving as a welcome spot for a post-round meal or a quick lunch between nines. Menu items typically include local Ehime specialties, such as *taimeshi* (sea bream rice), alongside classic golf course fare like curry rice, katsu-don, and udon noodles. The quality is consistent, and the prices reflect the course's "Value for Money" ethos, providing hearty meals without being overly expensive.

The dining area is often filled with local golfers, creating a relaxed and friendly atmosphere, especially during lunch hours. It's an ideal place to reflect on your round, enjoy a cold beverage, and perhaps try a local Ehime sake or beer. While not a gourmet destination, the food and beverage service meets expectations for a comfortable and enjoyable golf day experience.

## Caddie Tips

Playing Ehime Golf Club Main effectively requires more strategic thought than raw power, especially for those unfamiliar with mountain courses. A common mistake for many players is misjudging distances due to the significant elevation changes; uphill shots often require an extra club, while downhill shots typically need one less. Trusting your caddie or rangefinder with slope compensation is crucial here. Another pitfall is being overly aggressive off the tee on tight, tree-lined holes, leading to lost balls or punitive recovery shots from the forest.

Local knowledge, particularly regarding green reads and hidden hazards, is invaluable. Many greens have subtle breaks influenced by the surrounding mountain slopes, which can be hard to discern. An experienced caddie can point out the optimal landing areas on doglegs and identify the best lines for approaches to avoid the numerous bunkers and ravines. They can also offer insights into prevailing wind patterns that might affect shots on more exposed holes.

This course is particularly well-suited for mid to high-handicap golfers who enjoy a strategic challenge and appreciate natural beauty. Its "Value for Money" aspect makes it appealing for groups, couples, or solo travelers looking for an accessible and enjoyable round without breaking the bank. Low handicappers will also find a fair test of their precision and course management skills, as accuracy trumps sheer distance.

However, golfers who prefer wide-open, links-style courses or championship layouts designed for professional tours might find the tight fairways and constant elevation changes less to their liking. Ehime Golf Club Main rewards thoughtful shot-making, an appreciation for the mountain environment, and a relaxed approach to golf, making it an excellent choice for those seeking a true Japanese forest golf experience without excessive fanfare.
