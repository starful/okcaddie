## Course Overview
Kagoshima Kokusai Golf, established in 1974 and designed by Kazuo Kato, presents 27 holes across three distinct nines: Kirishima, Sakurajima, and Ibusuki. Playing an 18-hole combination typically yields a par of 72, with yardage from the back tees ranging around 6,700 to 6,900 yards. The course predominantly features Zoysia fairways and Bentgrass greens, characteristic of many well-maintained Japanese layouts. Its defining traits include significant elevation changes, panoramic views of the Kirishima mountains and Sakurajima volcano, and a layout that demands strategic shot-making rather than brute force.

## Layout & Strategy
Let's consider three representative holes that highlight the course's strategic demands. Kirishima Course, Hole 3, a downhill par 4, presents an inviting tee shot over a wide valley; however, a hidden bunker guards the landing zone on the right while the left side quickly runs into thick rough, making a precise hybrid or 3-wood the smarter play to set up a short iron to the green. The green itself slopes gently from back to front, often leading to tricky downhill putts if approached from above the hole.

Sakurajima Course, Hole 7, a testing par 5, features a sharp dogleg left around a dense forest. The brave can attempt to cut the corner with a powerful draw, risking out of bounds, while the safer route involves two well-placed lay-up shots to the right side of the fairway. The approach shot often plays significantly uphill to a two-tiered green, necessitating careful club selection to avoid short-siding yourself on the lower tier.

Ibusuki Course, Hole 5, a picturesque downhill par 3, demands an accurate iron over a small ravine to a green protected by bunkers front and back. The visual deception of the downhill shot often leads golfers to overclub; trusting the yardage and hitting one less club than usual is frequently the correct decision. Pay attention to the flag position, as the green features subtle undulations that funnel balls towards the center but can create deceptive breaking putts.

## Practical Info
Green fees at Kagoshima Kokusai Golf typically range from ¥8,000 to ¥14,000 on weekdays and ¥13,000 to ¥19,000 on weekends and holidays, usually including a shared cart. The course is publicly accessible, though advance reservations, particularly for weekend play, are highly recommended via online booking portals or travel agents. Play is predominantly cart-based, with electrically-powered, remotely-guided carts a common feature allowing for a relaxed pace. A standard golf dress code applies: collared shirts, tailored trousers or shorts, and soft spikes are expected, while denim is generally discouraged. Caddies are available upon request and for an additional fee, often compulsory for groups of four or specific peak times.

## Access
The course is conveniently located approximately a 45-minute drive north of Kagoshima City, the regional hub, making it accessible for visitors staying there. From Kagoshima-Chuo Station, follow National Route 10 north towards Aira. The nearest major train station is Aira Station on the JR Nippo Main Line, though a taxi or rental car from there is necessary for the final leg. Ample, complimentary parking is available on-site for those arriving by private or rental vehicle.

## Clubhouse Notes
The clubhouse offers functional and well-maintained facilities, including spacious locker rooms equipped with individual changing areas. A traditional Japanese communal bath (ofuro) provides a soothing post-round experience, featuring separate male and female sections, though it is not a natural onsen in the traditional sense. The restaurant, overlooking parts of the course, serves a range of local Kagoshima specialties and standard Japanese golf course fare; look for dishes featuring Kurobuta pork or local seafood for an authentic culinary treat.

## Tips
For optimal playing conditions, consider visiting during spring (April-May) or autumn (October-November) when temperatures are mild and the scenery is particularly vibrant. Summer can be humid and hot, while winter brings cooler, sometimes damp, weather, though the course remains open year-round. A common mistake here is underestimating the elevation changes and their impact on club selection, so trust your caddie or rangefinder. Keep an eye on weather forecasts, as mountain courses can experience sudden changes, including afternoon showers or strong winds. Expect higher green fees and more crowded tee sheets during peak Japanese holiday seasons like Golden Week or Obon.
