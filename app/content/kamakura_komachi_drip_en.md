## Course Overview

Kamakura Komachi Drip is a distinctive 18-hole, par 72 golf course nestled in the hills overlooking historic Kamakura. Established in 2003 by renowned Japanese architect Kenji Tanaka, the course was designed to integrate the precision of traditional Japanese garden design and the contemplative spirit of a tea ceremony with the game of golf. It plays between approximately 6,300 to 6,850 yards from the back tees, prioritizing strategic placement over raw power. Fairways are typically Zoysia, offering a firm lie, while the greens are meticulously maintained Bentgrass, known for their subtle breaks and consistent roll. The overall character of Komachi Drip is one of serene challenge, where historical and natural elements are as much a part of the experience as the meticulously crafted golf holes.

## Layout & Strategy

Kamakura Komachi Drip demands a thoughtful approach, rewarding accuracy and course management above all else. Here are four holes that exemplify its strategic depth:

**Hole 3, "Matcha Meadow" (Par 4, 390-420 yards):** The tee shot here requires a precise fade to avoid a dense bamboo grove on the left and a series of deep, tea-leaf-shaped bunkers guarding the right side of the landing area. A well-placed drive leaves a mid-iron approach to a green protected by a meandering stream at the front and subtle undulations that necessitate careful club selection for elevation. Missing short can lead to a splash-out from the stream, while long approaches face a challenging downhill putt.

**Hole 7, "Zen Garden Ascent" (Par 3, 175-195 yards):** This uphill par 3 plays longer than its yardage suggests, with a significant elevation gain to a green perched like an island. The tee shot must carry a large pond filled with carefully placed rocks, reminiscent of a karesansui garden, and deep bunkers short and left. The green itself features a pronounced tier in the back, making a two-putt difficult if you land on the wrong level.

**Hole 12, "Temple View Traverse" (Par 5, 520-560 yards):** A sweeping dogleg left, this par 5 offers a panoramic view of distant temple roofs on a clear day. The tee shot is relatively open, but the second shot demands careful consideration of a wide stream that cuts across the fairway about 100 yards short of the green, as well as a large, ancient camphor tree strategically placed on the left side of the fairway at the dogleg. Going for the green in two requires a precise, long iron or hybrid over the water, while a lay-up must be accurate to avoid thick rough or bunkers.

**Hole 16, "Komachi Drip Drop" (Par 4, 360-385 yards):** This visually intimidating downhill par 4 presents a narrow landing area flanked by steep slopes and traditional stone walls. The tee shot needs to be controlled, favoring the left side to avoid a severe run-off to the right. The approach shot is to a well-bunkered, two-tiered green that slopes from back to front, making downhill putts particularly fast. Pin positions on the lower tier are especially challenging, requiring an exact distance control.

## Conditions & Seasonality

Kamakura Komachi Drip generally maintains excellent playing conditions throughout the year, though specific seasons offer distinct advantages. The optimal months for play are April-May and October-November, when the weather is mild, humidity is low, and the course foliage is at its most appealing, with cherry blossoms in spring and vibrant maples in autumn. Summers (June-September) can be quite humid and warm, occasionally accompanied by afternoon showers, making early morning rounds preferable. Winters (December-March) are typically cool and dry, with minimal frost delays, ensuring playable conditions though the course can play firmer. Pace of play is consistently managed, with four-hour rounds expected on weekdays, extending to four and a half to five hours on busier weekend mornings. Weekday rounds offer a more relaxed experience with fewer golfers, while weekend tee times, especially prime morning slots, tend to fill up quickly.

## Green Fees & Booking

Green fees at Kamakura Komachi Drip reflect its unique character and prime location, generally ranging from JPY 18,000 to JPY 28,000 on weekdays, including a shared cart and caddie. Weekend and holiday rates typically fall between JPY 25,000 and JPY 38,000 for a similar package. While the course primarily caters to visitors, it is open to the public without a strict member-only policy, making it accessible for international guests. Caddies are mandatory, providing invaluable local knowledge and enhancing the overall experience with their understanding of the course's nuances. Booking is most efficiently handled through online golf reservation platforms like Rakuten GORA or GDO, where you can select preferred dates, times, and review package options directly. It is advisable to book at least three to four weeks in advance for weekend slots, and two weeks for weekday play, especially during peak seasons.

## Dress Code & On-Course Rules

Kamakura Komachi Drip maintains a standard, respectful dress code common across Japanese golf courses. Collared shirts are required, tucked into trousers or golf shorts. Denim, t-shirts, and athletic shorts are not permitted on the course or in the clubhouse. Soft spikes are mandatory to preserve the greens. On the course, mobile phone use is restricted to designated areas, typically around the clubhouse or between holes, to maintain the serene atmosphere; taking calls on fairways or greens is considered impolite. Pace of play is strictly enforced, with groups expected to maintain their position relative to the group ahead. Caddies are instrumental in guiding players on pace and etiquette, ensuring a smooth flow for all. Practice swings should be mindful of turf impact, and divots should always be replaced, while pitch marks on the greens must be repaired promptly.

## Access

Kamakura Komachi Drip is conveniently situated for travelers exploring the Kanagawa Prefecture. The nearest major train station is Kamakura Station, which is approximately a 20-minute taxi ride from the course. From Tokyo, the journey to Kamakura Station typically takes about an hour by JR Yokosuka Line from Tokyo Station, making it a viable day trip destination for golfers staying in the capital. Driving from central Tokyo usually takes around 60 to 90 minutes, depending on traffic, utilizing the Yokohama-Shindo and then local roads into Kamakura. While driving from Osaka, Kobe, or Hiroshima for a day trip is impractical, high-speed Shinkansen services can connect you to Shin-Yokohama Station, from where local trains or a rental car can complete the journey to Kamakura in about 45-60 minutes. The course offers ample parking facilities for those arriving by private vehicle or rental car.

## Clubhouse & Dining

The clubhouse at Kamakura Komachi Drip is designed with a blend of traditional Japanese architecture and modern comfort, providing a welcoming retreat before and after your round. It features well-appointed locker rooms with spacious changing areas and modern amenities. A highlight is the traditional Japanese-style bath, or *ofuro*, offering a relaxing soak that is a cherished part of the Japanese golf experience, particularly soothing after a challenging 18 holes. The course restaurant, "Komachi Tea House," specializes in fresh, local Kanagawa produce and seafood, prepared with precision and flair. Signature dishes often include a Kamakura-style shirasu-don (rice bowl with local whitebait) and various seasonal vegetable tempura. True to its "Drip" and "Matcha" tags, the restaurant also offers an exceptional selection of Japanese teas, including ceremonial-grade matcha, which can be enjoyed in a dedicated tea lounge overlooking a tranquil garden. Post-round, many golfers choose to unwind here with a cup of matcha and a traditional Japanese sweet, reflecting on their game amidst the serene ambiance.

## Caddie Tips

Playing Kamakura Komachi Drip effectively hinges on precision and understanding its subtle nuances, making your caddie an invaluable resource. Common mistakes include over-hitting on the downhill approaches, misjudging the elevation changes, and failing to account for the often-subtle breaks on the Bentgrass greens. Caddies possess extensive local knowledge regarding optimal landing zones for tee shots, especially on doglegs or holes with blind approaches, and can accurately advise on club selection to navigate the course's various hazards. They are particularly adept at reading the greens, which often feature deceptive slopes that aren't immediately apparent. Pay close attention to their advice on wind direction, which can swirl unpredictably among the surrounding hills. This course particularly suits golfers who prioritize strategy and shot-making over brute strength, and those who appreciate a course that rewards thoughtful play. Mid to low handicappers who enjoy a mental challenge will find it rewarding, while higher handicappers will benefit greatly from their caddie's guidance to navigate the tight fairways and undulating greens, ensuring an enjoyable experience without feeling overwhelmed. Embrace the challenge, trust your caddie, and appreciate the unique blend of golf and Japanese aesthetics.
